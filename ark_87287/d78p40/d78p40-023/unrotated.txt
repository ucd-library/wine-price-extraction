THE WINES OF GERMANY

[he news from Germany about the 1963 vintage carries little promise. Among the Rhine wines, we are

           

beginning to receive the fresh, graceful ’61s, and now there will be the very good ’62s—Rhines similar in
character to 1961, light and dry—but on the whole, better.

As for Moselle, the ’61s are light, dry, typical and satisfying, without being powerful. Unlike the Rhines,
the Moselles did not fare well in 1962. On the whole, the ’62s are thin, low in alcohol, too much acidity,
and many had to be sugared.

And while they last—and they will last—by all means acquire your ’59s while you can. Certainly the best
year in the last two decades, and perhaps the equal of the famed 1921. Many have been consumed before
they were ready—now is the time to try them, when they have sufficient bottle age to achieve balance and
bouquet. They will appear much less sweet today than two years ago. And the better 59s will certainly
improve for another five years. This is true of both the Rhines and the Moselles—ignore the lesser wines
which have passed their peak, and choose among the great estate-bottlings for some of the best white wines
of this generation.
